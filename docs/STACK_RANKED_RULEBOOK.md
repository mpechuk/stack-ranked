## Quick Start

New to Synergy Corp? Here's the entire game in four steps. Everything else in
this rulebook is detail.

1. **Stand-Up Meeting**: collect Productivity and Political Capital from your
   tableau (plus 1 free Productivity just for showing up). Then pick up one
   more Project card from the Kanban Board into your Backlog — you always
   take one, every Stand-Up, no matter how many you're already sitting on;
   you're just claiming it, not paying for it yet.
2. **Sprint**: spend your Action Points to Hire a Skill card, Work a Project
   (pay to finish any one entry in your Backlog — your choice which), Network,
   or Self-Care. You may also take one Overtime action per round for a bonus
   Action Point.
3. **Lunch**: draw an Office Chaos card and resolve it.
4. **Postmortem**: refill the boards and pass the First Player token. Every
   3rd round is a Quarterly Performance Review — the top performer(s) may get
   promoted, the bottom performer(s) get a warning.

First player to be promoted into CEO wins. That's it — the rest is how to get
there without burning out.

## Welcome to Synergy Corp

You started in the mailroom — metaphorically, or possibly literally, HR was
vague about it. Somewhere above you, seven layers of management stand between
you and the corner office, and every one of them got there the same way
you're about to try: performance reviews, strategic networking, and a truly
heroic tolerance for mandatory training videos.

Synergy Corp doesn't make anything anyone can quite explain. It disrupts. It
pivots. It once acquired a competitor specifically to shut down the product
line customers actually liked. None of that is your problem. Your problem is
Career Capital, Political Capital, and not having a Burnout Crisis in front
of the VP of Product.

### Objective

Climb the Career Ladder from Intern to CEO before anyone else does. You'll do
this by completing Projects and building a professional network — both feed
your Career Capital, the permanent score that determines whether you're
actually eligible for promotion. Everything else — Skill cards, Political
Capital, even your quarterly Review Score — exists to help (or hurt) that
climb.

## Key Concepts

### The Three Live Resources

**Productivity (P).** Your output. Spend it to Hire Skill/Tool cards and to
Work Projects. Productivity does not carry Career Capital by itself — it's
what you convert into Career Capital.

**Political Capital (PC).** Your visibility and relationships. Gained mainly
through Networking. Spent on nothing directly — it's compared at every
Performance Review and decides the CEO Board Vote, so hoarding it is a
legitimate strategy.

**Burnout (0–10).** Your risk track. Rises from Overtime, some Skill cards,
some Projects, and Office Chaos. At 10, you suffer a Burnout Crisis: reset to
6, lose 2 Political Capital, and skip your next Sprint entirely.

### The Two Permanent Trackers

**Career Capital (CC).** Your real, permanent résumé score. It only ever goes
up — fed by completed Projects (a lot) and Networking (a little, but
steadily). Every promotion is gated by a Career Capital threshold. Nothing
ever removes it.

**Compliance Badges.** A minor counter from Mandatory Training. Director
requires 2 Badges; VP requires 4. Nobody has ever been promoted primarily
because of their Badges, but you cannot be promoted without them.

### The Quarter Marker

Each player has a second cube that sits on their Career Capital track,
marking where their CC was as of the last Performance Review. The gap between
your current CC and your Quarter Marker is how much you've actually grown
this Quarter — and it's the heart of your Review Score (see
[Quarterly Performance Review](#quarterly-performance-review)). Move your
Quarter Marker to match your current CC at the end of every Review.

### Your Backlog

Your Backlog is every Project you've picked up but haven't paid for yet.
There's no limit to how many you can hold. Every Stand-Up, you take one
more card from the Kanban Board into your Backlog — always, whether you're
currently sitting on zero or a dozen (see
[6.1](#61-stand-up-meeting-income-phase)). Taking a task is free; paying its
Productivity cost is what actually finishes it and earns the reward, and
that only happens on your Sprint, via Work a Project (see
[6.2](#62-sprint-action-phase)) — Stand-Up only ever adds to your Backlog,
never lets you pay for anything in it. When you Work a Project, you choose
which Backlog entry to pay off — cheapest, most valuable, whatever suits
you — and if you have enough Action Points and Productivity, you can clear
more than one in the same Sprint. Ignore your Backlog for too long, though,
and it just keeps growing.

## Setup

1. Place the Career Ladder board where everyone can reach it. Each player
   takes a Desk mat, a pawn (placed on Intern), and starts with 0
   Productivity, 0 Political Capital, 0 Burnout, 0 Career Capital, 0
   Compliance Badges, and an empty Backlog.
2. Shuffle the Management Style deck. Each player draws 1 card and keeps it
   face-up — everyone should be able to see everyone's boss.
3. Shuffle the Tier 1 Skill/Tool cards and reveal 5 to form the Job Board.
4. Shuffle the Early Project cards and reveal 4 to form the Kanban Board,
   alongside a card drawn from the 13-card evergreen pool (headlined by
   Reduce Technical Debt) as a permanent 5th slot.
5. Shuffle the Office Chaos deck and the Mandatory Training deck separately.
   Place both face-down within reach.
6. Give each player a Quarter Marker cube and place it on Career Capital
   position 0, right alongside their main tracker.
7. Determine the first player: whoever had the most recent performance review
   in real life goes first. If nobody can agree, whoever currently holds the
   most impressive-sounding made-up job title takes the token.

## How to Play a Round

A round has four phases, always in this order. Every player resolves each
phase before anyone moves to the next.

### 6.1 — Stand-Up Meeting (Income Phase)

Simultaneously, every player collects the Productivity and Political Capital
shown on their Skill/Tool tableau, plus a flat +1 Productivity "showed up"
bonus.

Then, in First Player order (this part isn't simultaneous — everyone's
reaching for the same Kanban Board): every player takes one Project card
from the Kanban Board into their Backlog — your choice of any card
currently on the board. This happens every single Stand-Up, for everyone,
no matter how large a Backlog you're already carrying — there's no cap and
no "only if you're empty-handed" exception. This is free; you are not
paying its cost yet, just claiming it. Taking it empties that slot exactly
as if you'd completed a Project there (a regular slot stays empty until the
next Postmortem refills it; the Evergreen slot instantly deals itself a
replacement — see [Deck Composition](#deck-composition) — which is why
there's always something to pick up even if every other slot is already
spoken for this Stand-Up). Whatever cost is showing on the card at the
moment you take it (including any Scope Creep it had already picked up) is
locked in for that entry — it won't creep any higher while it sits in your
Backlog, even though the slot that replaces it will start accruing its own
Scope Creep from scratch.

Stand-Up never lets you pay for anything in your Backlog — that only
happens on your Sprint, via Work a Project.

### 6.2 — Sprint (Action Phase)

In First Player order, each player spends Action Points one at a time. Your
Action Point budget depends on your current rung:

| Rung | Action Points |
|---|---|
| Intern / Software Engineer | 2 |
| Team Lead / Manager | 3 |
| Director / VP / CEO | 4 |

Each Action Point buys one of the following:

- **Hire** — pay a Skill/Tool card's Productivity cost to take it from the
  Job Board. One-Shot cards resolve immediately and are discarded; Permanent
  cards join your tableau.
- **Work a Project** — choose any one entry in your Backlog (see
  [Your Backlog](#your-backlog)) and pay its Productivity cost. This is the
  only way to actually pay for and finish a task — Stand-Up only ever adds
  to your Backlog, never pays for anything in it. Gain the Career Capital
  reward (and any other listed effects) immediately, then that entry is
  gone. You choose which entry to Work — cheapest, most valuable, whatever
  — and if you have enough Action Points and Productivity, you can Work
  more than one entry in the same Sprint. Unavailable only if your Backlog
  is completely empty (rare — Stand-Up adds an entry every round).
- **Network** — no cost. Gain 2 Political Capital and 1 Career Capital.
- **Self-Care** — no cost. Remove 2 Burnout.

Once per round, before or after your normal Action Points, you may also take
Overtime: gain 1 extra Action Point to spend this round, but gain 2 Burnout.

#### Scope Creep

Any Project that sits unclaimed on the board for 2 full rounds has its
Productivity cost increased by 1 (its reward never changes). The deadline
moves. The budget doesn't. This does not apply to the Kanban Board's
Evergreen slot.

### 6.3 — Lunch

Draw the top card of the Office Chaos deck and resolve it exactly as written.

### 6.4 — Postmortem

Refill the Job Board and Kanban Board back up to their full size, and pass
the First Player token clockwise. Every 2nd Quarterly Review (after rounds 6,
12, 18, and so on), before the next round's Stand-Up Meeting, every player
simultaneously draws and resolves a Mandatory Training card.

## Quarterly Performance Review

Every 3rd round (rounds 3, 6, 9…) is a Quarterly Performance Review. Resolve
these steps in order:

### Step 1 — Calculate Review Score

Review Score = (Career Capital gained since your Quarter Marker) + (Political
Capital on hand) − (Burnout ÷ 4, rounded down).

This rewards real output delivered this Quarter and current visibility — not
just whoever happens to be holding the most cash at the buzzer.

### Step 2 — CEO Board Vote

Any player at VP whose Career Capital is 78 or more is a Board Vote
candidate. If there is exactly one candidate, they become CEO immediately —
the game ends after this round. If there are multiple candidates, whoever
holds the most Political Capital on hand wins the vote and becomes CEO. (Yes:
a VP who was never anyone's top performer can still win the vote and take the
corner office. Several early playtest groups found this personally upsetting.
We left it in.)

### Step 3 — Standard Promotions

Determine promotion slots for your player count (see
[Player Count Adjustments](#player-count-adjustments)). Among players who
meet their next rung's Career Capital requirement (and Badge requirement, if
any) — excluding anyone already crowned CEO this Review — the highest Review
Score(s), up to the number of slots, are promoted one rung.

Eligible players who aren't selected this time don't lose anything. Instead
they become Employee of the Quarter: gain 1 Political Capital immediately and
keep a token (worth bonus points in the Advanced Variant — see
[Winning the Game](#winning-the-game)).

#### Meteoric Rise

If a promoted player's Review Score is at least double the next-highest
Review Score, and they also meet the Career Capital (and Badge) requirement
for the rung after that, they leap two rungs instead of one. This cannot
leapfrog directly into CEO — the Board Vote is the only door to the top.

### Step 4 — PIP and Demotion

Among players not promoted or crowned this Review, the lowest Review
Score(s) — up to the PIP slot count — receive a PIP token. A player who
already holds a PIP token instead loses it and is demoted one rung. A player
being demoted from Intern instead enters Freelance Purgatory: skip the entire
next Quarter's Sprints (you still draw Office Chaos and Training), then
return to Intern with a clean slate.

### Step 5 — Reset

Every player moves their Quarter Marker to match their current Career
Capital. Burnout, Career Capital, and Compliance Badges persist. Anyone
promoted or demoted draws a new Management Style card.

### Mentorship Bonus

Any player whose rung is 2 or more behind the current leader gains +1
Political Capital every Stand-Up Meeting, until they close the gap. Synergy
Corp calls this a mentorship program. It is, mechanically, a rubber band.

## Management Styles

At setup, and again every time you're promoted or demoted, you draw a
Management Style card and keep it face-up on your Desk mat. It's a passive,
asymmetric power that lasts until your next promotion or demotion — for
better or (usually) worse.

Full text for all 10 Management Style cards is in the
[Card Reference Appendix](#card-reference-appendix). A few highlights:

- The Actually Supportive Manager is the only card in the deck with no
  drawback. Everyone will be suspicious of whoever draws it.
- The Micromanager and The Founder Who Refuses to Delegate both look like
  upgrades and both come with a catch that shows up on your very next turn.
- Getting promoted or demoted always means a new boss. There is no version of
  this game where you keep the manager you liked.

## Winning the Game

The base game ends the instant a player is crowned CEO via the Board Vote
(finish the current round for fairness, then stop). That player wins.

### Advanced Variant: The Long Game

For a more strategic, less race-focused experience, play a fixed 8 Quarters
(24 rounds) instead of racing to CEO. At the end of Round 24, calculate a
Final Score for each player:

Final Score = (Rung × 10) + (Career Capital ÷ 2) + (Political Capital on
hand) − Burnout + (5 × Employee-of-the-Quarter tokens).

Highest Final Score wins — even if nobody ever reaches CEO. This variant
rewards consistent, well-rounded play over a single lucky Board Vote.

## Player Count Adjustments

### 2 Players

Play proceeds exactly as normal — 1 promotion slot, 1 PIP slot. With only one
rival, the promotion race gets noticeably more personal (and more petty).

### 6 Players

Use 2 promotion slots and 2 PIP slots per Review (already reflected in the
Career Ladder table). Additionally, reveal a second row of 5 Job Board cards
(10 total) so the market doesn't dry up with more players competing for the
same Skill cards.

## Designer's Notes: Balancing Stack Ranked

This game was tuned the same way you'd tune any economy: build bots that play
fixed, extreme strategies, run tens of thousands of games, look at what
breaks, and fix it. Three iterations were run, 20,000 games each, always with
one of each of five archetypes seated at the same table — Grinder (projects
only), Politician (networking only), Workaholic (projects plus reckless
Overtime), Cautious (safety-first generalist), and Balanced (does a bit of
everything). All numbers below are the actual output of the simulator
included with this game (simulate.py), not hand-picked estimates.

### v1 — The Baseline (and its very broken AND-gate)

The first version gated every promotion behind two completely independent
lifetime stats: cumulative Productivity ever spent on Projects, AND
cumulative Political Capital ever earned through Networking. Both had to
individually clear a threshold. It seemed reasonable on paper.

It was not reasonable in practice. Grinder never networks, so its lifetime
Political Capital stat sat at zero forever — permanently ineligible for any
promotion no matter how many Projects it finished. Politician never works a
Project, so its lifetime Productivity stat stayed at zero — same problem,
opposite resource. Workaholic, despite its extra Action Points, also never
networks and was equally stuck. Three of five archetypes were mathematically
incapable of ever being promoted.

Result: Balanced won 68.0% of games and Cautious won 32.0% — the only two
archetypes that ever touch both resources. Grinder, Politician, and
Workaholic won a combined 0.0%. Games also dragged: an average of 66.8 rounds,
because for most of the game only two of the five players at the table could
legally advance at all.

There's an accidental joke buried in this bug that we liked enough to think
about seriously: the simulation kept showing Grinder as the top quarterly
scorer by raw output, then getting passed over for promotion anyway because
it had never once networked. We didn't plan that. Stack ranking did that to
itself.

### v2 — Unify the Gate (partial fix)

The obvious repair: stop requiring two independent stats and replace them
with one unified Career Capital track, fed by both completed Projects (a lot
per completion) and Networking (a little, but repeatable), with a fallback so
a promotion slot checks the next-highest scorer instead of vanishing when the
top scorer doesn't qualify.

Result: Politician climbed from mathematically impossible to a real, if weak,
4.6%. Average game length dropped from 67 rounds to 32.5, since Career
Capital now accumulates from two directions instead of one. But Grinder and
Workaholic were still stuck at 0.0% and 0.0% — unifying the gate hadn't fixed
them at all.

Digging into the per-round logs turned up a second, subtler bug: the
Quarterly Review Score used to decide who gets the (limited) promotion slot
only counted leftover banked Productivity and Political Capital — and a pure
producer spends essentially all of their Productivity buying Projects, every
single round. Grinder and Workaholic were quietly banking more Career Capital
than anyone else at the table while posting the worst quarterly "optics" of
anyone, every single Quarter, because their bank balance always looked empty
right when it mattered. They were never being blocked by the gate anymore —
they just never got a turn at it.

### v3 — Fix the Scorecard, Rein In Overtime, Add a Safety Net

Three changes went into the final pass:

- Review Score now explicitly counts Career Capital earned since your
  Quarter Marker, not just leftover cash on hand — so completing Projects
  shows up on your review even after you've spent the Productivity.
- Overtime's Burnout cost rose from 1 to 2, specifically to stop Workaholic
  from chaining Overtime every single round with no real downside.
- A Mentorship Bonus (+1 Political Capital per round) now kicks in for
  anyone 2+ rungs behind the leader, and Networking's Career Capital
  contribution was increased so a dedicated networker isn't hopelessly
  outpaced by project-driven Career Capital lump sums.

Result: Politician reached a genuinely playable 10.7%. Grinder and Workaholic
finally register real wins (0.5% and 1.4%) instead of a mathematical zero.
Balanced and Cautious remain the strongest all-around approaches at 51.3% and
36.1%, and we think that's correct, not a flaw: a game about corporate
ascension where the specialist who ignores politics entirely, or the one who
ignores real output entirely, wins as often as the well-rounded player would
be a strange piece of satire to ship.

### Other Numbers Worth Knowing

| Metric | v1 | v2 | v3 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Avg. game length (rounds) | 66.8 | 32.5 | 34.4 |
| Games ending via real CEO promotion | 99.8% | 100.0% | 100.0% |
| Avg. Burnout Crises per game | 2.79 | 1.21 | 2.27 |
| Board Vote "upsets"* | 18.0% | 0.1% | 0.0% |

\* An "upset" is a game where the eventual CEO wasn't the first player to
reach VP. v1's very long, two-horse-race games saw this 18% of the time; once
games sped up and cleared the scoring bug in v2–v3, whichever generalist got
to VP first now almost always wins the vote before a rival VP catches up.

A pacing check across player counts (v3 rules, 5,000 games each, strategies
drawn randomly): 2 players averaged 32.5 rounds, 3 players 31.4, 4 players
32.7, and 6 players 30.2 — all landing comfortably in the 60–90 minute target
with real human decision time, and 100% of games in every player count ended
via an actual CEO promotion rather than hitting the round cap.

### An Honest Caveat

These bots each follow one fixed, exaggerated policy for the entire game —
that's what makes them useful for stress-testing the extremes. A real player
is not a fixed policy. Nothing stops you from grinding Projects for the first
half of the game and pivoting hard into Networking once you can see who's
about to hit the Board Vote threshold. We'd expect a table of skilled humans
to find the specialist strategies more viable than these numbers suggest —
but if you want a genuinely hard mode, try playing Grinder or Workaholic as
written and see how it goes. That's what the simulation says it's like.

## Quick Reference Sheet

### Round Structure

1. Stand-Up Meeting — collect tableau income + 1 free Productivity; pick up
   one more task from the Kanban Board into your Backlog (free — claim
   only, no paying yet; happens every round, no cap)
2. Sprint — spend Action Points (2 / 3 / 4 by rung); 1 free Overtime action
   available
3. Lunch — draw & resolve 1 Office Chaos card
4. Postmortem — refill boards, pass First Player token

Every 3rd round: Quarterly Performance Review. Every 6th round: also resolve
Mandatory Training.

### Actions (1 AP each unless noted)

| Action | Cost | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Hire | Card's Productivity cost | Take a Job Board card: One-Shot resolves now; Permanent joins tableau |
| Work a Project | Chosen Backlog entry's Productivity cost | Gain its Career Capital reward + listed effects; that entry leaves your Backlog |
| Network | Free | +2 Political Capital, +1 Career Capital |
| Self-Care | Free | −2 Burnout |
| Overtime (once/round, not an AP) | +2 Burnout | +1 Action Point this round |

### Your Backlog

No size limit — every Stand-Up you take one more card into it, whether
you're empty or overflowing (free — claim only). Paying for any entry and
taking its reward only happens on your Sprint, via Work a Project (1 AP per
entry) — Stand-Up never completes anything for you. You choose which entry
to Work each time, and can clear more than one per Sprint if you have the
AP and Productivity for it.

### Review Score

(Career Capital gained since your Quarter Marker) + (Political Capital on
hand) − (Burnout ÷ 4, rounded down)

### Burnout Crisis (at 10)

Reset to 6. Lose 2 Political Capital. Skip your next Sprint.
